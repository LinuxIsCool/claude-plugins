Today, most compliance review remains manual, slow, and reactive, forcing architects and builders into costly revision cycles after plans are already submitted.

This is the gap Ulama is designed to address.

By tightening the feedback loop between design and regulation, Ulama can significantly reduce permitting timelines, one of the most overlooked constraints in the global housing supply chain. At scale, faster approvals do more than save time and money. They unlock the ability to build more housing, more predictably, in cities and regions facing acute shortages.

The pressure to build has never been higher. Housing shortages have become a defining economic and political issue, driving up costs, constraining labor mobility, and reshaping local economies. In response, governments are actively searching for ways to increase supply without compromising safety, accessibility, or environmental standards.

One visible signal of this shift is the growing influence of YIMBY policies. Support for building more housing now cuts across political lines, with cities and states experimenting with zoning reform, density allowances, and faster approvals. The common thread is clear. Policymakers want housing projects to move from concept to construction more quickly.

At the same time, the permitting process has not meaningfully evolved. Even as regulations change upstream, building code compliance remains complex, fragmented, and largely manual. Architects and builders are still forced into reactive workflows where violations are discovered only after submission, triggering costly revisions and long delays. 

This is a costly process as a whole: the global cost of permit activities itself is $700B according to the Ulama team!

This tension between pro-housing policy and outdated permitting infrastructure creates a critical opportunity. As more projects enter the pipeline, the cost of inefficiency compounds. Faster zoning approvals lose their impact if permitting remains slow and unpredictable.

So, as housing, infrastructure, and regulatory complexity rise together, Ulamaâ€™s ability to empower builders to build efficiently without lowering standards is a competitive advantage.

Ulama is addressing a real and costly failure point in the construction lifecycle. Permitting friction is not a surface level inconvenience. It is a structural constraint that compounds risk, delays capital deployment, and limits housing supply. A product that meaningfully reduces this friction has the potential to become core infrastructure rather than a nice to have tool.

From a scalability standpoint, Ulama is well positioned. Its SaaS delivery model allows it to expand across geographies without the linear cost structure of traditional compliance services. Its integration into Revit is particularly important, as Revit is already embedded in the daily workflows of the vast majority of architectural firms. This positions Ulama as an extension of existing behavior rather than a tool that requires teams to change how they work.

There are other companies operating in adjacent parts of the market, most notably Archistar, which has raised more than thirty five million Australian dollars to date. However, Ulama appears differentiated in how directly it embeds compliance into the act of design itself. Rather than functioning as an external review layer, Ulama focuses on surfacing issues in real time so architects can resolve them before submission. That shift from review to prevention is meaningful.

There are also clear challenges ahead. Granting third party software access to private architectural designs raises legitimate security and trust concerns, particularly for firms working on sensitive or high value projects. Additionally, building codes vary not only by region but often by interpretation, creating ongoing complexity in maintaining accurate and current rule sets.

These challenges are inseparable from the size of the opportunity. If Ulama can build trust around data security and demonstrate reliable coverage across jurisdictions, it has the potential to become a foundational layer in how buildings are designed and approved.** In a world where pressure to build faster continues to rise, software that enables speed without sacrificing standards stands to win long term.**
